# Tomato Discovery Toy Product Vision Prompt

Create a 400 to 600 word product vision and use case for an incredibly unique, viral tomato discovery experience. This is not a commercial site, shopping tool, startup pitch, or ordinary catalog. It should feel like a fascinating interactive toy: a museum-quality tomato atlas, a sensory playground, a seasonal cooking oracle, and a beautifully restrained discovery interface merged into one experience. Even people who do not like tomatoes should enjoy exploring it because the interface itself is strange, tactile, visual, and satisfying.

The product must catalog as many meaningful tomato varieties as possible and classify each by 40 to 60 attributes. Organize the attributes intelligently across flavor, culinary behavior, physical form, horticulture, origin, seasonality, rarity, and visual appeal. Include traits such as sweetness, acidity, bitterness, umami, aroma, juiciness, water content, flesh density, skin thickness, seediness, size, color, shape, ribbing, texture, shelf life, sauce performance, slicing quality, roasting quality, canning suitability, salad use, sandwich use, salsa use, disease resistance, heat tolerance, cold tolerance, crack resistance, days to maturity, best growing region, climate zone, seasonality, heirloom or hybrid status, origin, rarity, seed availability, and ideal meal pairing.

The homepage should not be a marketing landing page. It should open directly into the toy: a full-screen, elegant, tactile tomato finder. The first screen should immediately invite play through unusual controls: four-way axis sliders, precision adjusters, bitter-to-sweet balancing, location-aware filtering, time-of-year recommendations, a "Perfect Tomato Finder," and a meal selector where the user chooses what they are preparing: sauce, salad, sandwich, salsa, pizza, soup, shakshuka, canning batch, roasted dish, fine-dining plate, or fresh snack. As users move through flavor, shape, season, or meal intent, tomatoes should appear to morph into other tomatoes with a special visual effect, making the database feel alive: colors shift, silhouettes swell or flatten, ribbing emerges, and the recommended variety resolves like a specimen being discovered.

The interface should make a huge database feel simple, fast, and delightful. A user should be able to arrive with either a practical need, such as "best tomato for pizza sauce in July in New Jersey," or a sensory curiosity, such as "show me dark, smoky, ribbed tomatoes that look like alien fruit," and immediately get beautiful results. The experience should reward dragging, comparing, sorting, flipping, filtering, wandering, and watching one tomato transform into the next. It should feel playful without becoming childish, and refined without becoming cold.

Use Wikimedia Commons as the preferred image source for every tomato variety where available. Images must feel real and inspectable, not generic. Each tomato card should feel collectible and shareable: a strong photo, flavor profile, origin story, rarity score, growing notes, best culinary use, seasonality, and one surprising fact.

The tone should be premium, sensory, intelligent, educational, and quietly weird. Avoid generic startup language, monetization language, vague "AI-powered" claims, and bloated feature lists. Make the product feel specific, buildable, iconic, and worth sharing because it turns biodiversity, flavor, color, seasonality, and gardening knowledge into something people can play with.

Write the final output as a polished use case and product vision, not a technical spec. It should make a designer want to design it, a developer understand the interface, a chef trust the recommendations, a botanist respect the data model, and an ordinary visitor want to explore tomatoes for an hour just because the toy feels good to use.
